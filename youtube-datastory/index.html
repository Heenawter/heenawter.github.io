WHAT'S TRENDING?

A Look at 5 Months of YouTube Trending Videos

A data story by Hannah Wright.

What is the goal of this data story?

Have you ever wondered what it takes to make the trending page of YouTube? The goal of this data story is to answer this question as best as possible.

In order to accomplish this, I have two versions of the same dataset to consider: one which contains every single video that has trended since November 14th, 2017 and a sampled version. The sampled version reduces the number of rows from 116,738 to 34,110, and was created in order to accommodate for translation limitations. Specifically, in order to perform parts of the analysis to follow, the title column needed to be translated; since the Google API that was used for this task had a set character limit, the dataset had to be sampled in order for this to happen. In each question below, it will be clearly stated which dataset was used in order to perform the analysis.

For each figure that follows, if the name includes an asterisk (*), you are able to hover over the image in order to get an annotated or different version of that same figure.

Question #1

What genre should your video be?

The trending page of YouTube is broken down into 15 different categories. One possible subset of the more popular genres, in no particular order, is as follows:

  • Comedy
  • Gaming
  • Entertainment
  • Music
  • Howto & Style
  • Sports
With such a large variety of options to choose from, which category should you make content for? Obviously, the most obvious answer would be to make content for the genre you are passionate about and talented in. After all, if you are hopelessly tone deaf, it may not be worth your time to pursue a career in music. That being said, let us approach this problem as though each genre of video is equally likely and enjoyable. Considering this, what factors would influence our decision on which category to aim for?

One of the first things that comes to mind when hearing that a video is 'popular' on YouTube is likely the number of views a specific video gets. It is unlikely, for example, that a video with under 500 views would be considered 'popular' by the general YouTube audience. The number of views also impacts how much revenue a specific video will pull in, which is Important if you intend to make this YouTube thing a career. Considering these things, it makes sense to start by looking at views in order to determine which category is most likely to achieve the best results.

As can be seen above, the Music category far outweighs the other genres in terms of overall views. In comparison to the second most viewed category (Entertainment), Music has approximately 30-million more; this is nearly double the number of views! With some thought, this result is fairly unsurprising. First off, music is universally shareable. This is especially true due to the lack of a language barrier when it comes to music in comparison to other forms of digital media. Although you may not understand the lyrics of a particular song, that by no means impacts your ability to enjoy it. As a result, it is not uncommon for music videos to trend in multiple countries. For example, here's a small subset of the 55 different music videos that made the trending page of every country in our dataset:

'MIC Drop (Steve Aoki Remix)' Official MV

By BTS (방탄소년단)

End Game ft. Ed Sheeran, Future

By Taylor Swift

Man of the Woods (Official Video)

By Justin Timberlake
In comparison, the next most universally shared category was Entertainment with only 29 videos that made every trending page; this is almost half of the number of music videos that met the same condition. The category 'Film & Animation' had 15 of these videos, and all the remaining categories had 10 or less. Of the fifteen possible categories, 3 of them had the minimum value of 1. This helps to illustrate our idea that music videos are more universally shareable in comparison to the other categories.

Despite the Music category getting the most views overall, the future is not-so-bleak for the other categories. Figure 2 below illustrates the number of views (per day) music videos get in comparison to all other categories. It is worth mentioning that this figure was constructed using the sampled version of our dataset. This helped to normalize the results, since only 5 videos from each category (per day) were included.

Figure 2 shows that the views of the videos in the Music category as well as in all other categories (when considered as a whole) are on the rise. In fact, the views trend line for 'Other' seems to have a slightly steeper slope than the one for music videos. This shows that, while music videos may get more views overall than all other categories, views in general are on an upward trend. As a result, the future of views looks promising regardless of the video category you choose.

However, views are not all that matters when it comes to choosing a category that fits you best. For example, you may be someone who values user comments and direct communication with your audience more than simply the number of views. On the other hand, you may be heavily impacted by negative feedback, so you'd prefer to pick a category that is more likely to keep things positive. These are other factors, besides views, that may impact your decision of which category to produce content for.

Figure 3 below shows the breakdown of user interaction per category, as described by the average number of comments, likes, and dislikes on the videos. Since the views of every category far outweighed any other interaction factor (and since I've already heavily discussed views above), I chose not to include the percentage of user interaction that comes from views. This helped give more insight into the other factors.

Using Figure 3, we can answer the above hypothetical examples. If user comments are an important factor for you when making your videos, then the category 'Nonprofits & Activism' is for you. This can be seen by the fact that this category has 21% of its user interaction coming from comments, which is the most out of any other category. Interestingly, this category also has a higher than average percentage of dislikes, coming in at 14.5%. As a result, this may not be the best category for the second scenario where negative feedback is extremely undesirable. Instead, the best categories for this scenario would be 'Pets & Animals' (with only 1.7% of user interaction coming from dislikes) or 'Comedy' (which has only 2.7%).

Question #2

Which language should you speak?

Depending on the language you speak, it's possible that your video may not trend. After all, making a video in a language that does not fall into the majority limits the potential size of the audience for that video, which goes against the fundamental idea behind the trending page. Trending videos are meant to target as wide of an audience as possible, so it makes sense to publish a video in one of the top languages if you want to increase your chances of making this list.

Then, let's start by looking at the diversity of each trending page. In the context of this analysis, diversity is measured by the number of unique languages detected for the titles of each country's page. Overall, my sampled dataset had a total of 78 unique languages used for the titles while, in the full dataset, the number of unique languages is most likely much larger. Still, I was able to get a good understanding of the breakdown of the majority languages, and this analysis can be seen in Table 1.

In order to get a more in-depth analysis of the language use in each trending page, I took the top 5 languages from each country and mapped the percentage breakdown of the resulting shortened language list for each of the trending pages. Since there was a lot of overlap in these top 5 lists, I ended up only looking at 12 different languages:

  • English
  • French
  • Chinese
  • Hindi
  • German
  • Turkish
  • Russian
  • Arabic
  • Haitian Creole
  • Korean
  • Japanese
  • Portuguese
The percentage breakdown of each of these languages can be seen in Figure 4. Note that these percentages do not represent the overall percentages of titles on each trending page. Instead, I removed any video that was not in one of the above languages and did my analysis on this subset of data. As a result, the actual percentages for each language will be slightly smaller. However, this percentage difference was minimal when compared to the overall breakdown; so, I chose to sacrifice a small amount of accuracy in favour of readability by reducing the number of languages presented on each bar.

In both Table 1 and Figure 4, we can see that 3 out of 5 of the trending pages in my dataset had a majority of English videos. In fact, the United States and United Kingdom were both so English-dominated (over 95%) that the remaining percentages of languages are nearly invisible in the language breakdown. However, these results were not that far from my expectations.

In the UK, 92% of the population considered English to be their main language in 2013 according to the BBC [source]. The USA, on the other hand, has approximately 70% of their population made up of native English speakers [source]. That being said, it was slightly surprising that the American trending page was not slightly more diverse. Despite 11% people in the United States speaking Spanish natively, only 5 video titles in my sampled dataset were in Spanish. In comparison, France's dataset contained 28.

While it is not reasonable to draw a direct connection between the titles of YouTube's trending page and that country's actual diversity, I still found this result to be interesting. That being said, it is entirely possible that my sampling process somehow missed many of the non-English videos and thus skewed the results in one way or the other. As a result, these percentages are not actually representative of the languages spoken in a specific country. Regardless, this still presents an interesting look at the languages that dominated each trending page.

Next, I wanted to see if the trending pages of YouTube were becoming more or less dominated by English videos over time. So, I took the top 3 diverse countries from above (France, Germany, and Canada) and mapped their English use VS all other languages. The results for this can be seen in Figure 5 below. It is worth mentioning that I returned to the entire language list for this analysis unlike in the analysis above; hence, the 'Other' category consists of 76 different languages.

Figure 5 shows that the trend for English videos making the trending page is slightly positive whereas the 'Other' category seems to be on a gentle decline. While the 'Other' languages outweigh the English videos in this subset of the data on almost every day, there are 2 days where English overtakes the other languages: January 28th and March 5th in 2018. When searching for a cause for this, I found that the Grammy Awards aired on January 28th and the Oscars took place on March 4th (the day before March 5th); this may explain the increase in English videos on the trending page on these two days.

So, based on the above, it is fair to conclude that a video with a title in English is more likely to trend than titles in any other languages. Both French and German are also good choices, although you are less likely to trend globally with these languages as your title. Of the 23 videos that made every single trending page, only 1 of them was classified as non-English. However, upon further inspection of this video, it turns out it was actually Troye Sivan's music video for his song 'My My My!' Despite being in English, the title had been misclassified by the Google API as Russian. So, in reality, not a single one of these videos was non-English. Hence, English is still the best overall choice if you want to make the trending page.

As a reminder, I had to use the sampled version of my dataset in order to accommodate for translation limitations in this analysis question. It is also worth mentioning that I assumed the language used for the title represents the language used in the video itself. Obviously, this is not always the case. However, this still provides a decent approximation while still accommodating for my limited translation capabilities.

One other limitation of this analysis is that the Google API used for translation is not 100% accurate with its language detection. This was demonstrated above, with Troy Sivan's video being mislabeled as Russian despite being in English. However, there is not much that can be done to improve this limitation with today's tools; after all, Google Translate is one of the best tools on the market to perform this type of analysis.

Question #3

How should you title / tag your video?

One of the first things a potential viewer will see of your video is the title. Because of this, it's important to pick a title that will draw them in and make them want to click. Now, there are a variety of techniques that are designed to do just that. For example, clickbait, although disingenuous, is one method that is known to work.

According to the American Marketing Association, one video with a normal title received only 1-million views, whereas the same video, rebranded with a clickbait title, was able to pull in 17-million [source]. This is due to the information gap theory, which states that "humans have an insatiable appetite to indulge their curiosity" [source]. While clickbait is proven, we want to see if there are any other commonalities in the titles of trending videos.

For the analysis to follow, it is worth mentioning that I was once again limited to the sampled version of the dataset. Not only did I want to prevent biases towards English titles and words, I was also unsure of the reliability of my sentiment analysis tool for non-English words. In order to keep things as equal as I could, I chose to stick to my translated titles rather than the full dataset.

Let's start by looking at the most used words in the titles of these trending videos (excluding stopwords such as 'the,' which provide no insight). Beyond that, let's examine whether words of a specific sentiment are more common in titles than others. While making a title using these specific words will by no means guarantee trending status, it will provide an insight on what kinds of words may increase your chances.

The results of this analysis are shown in Figure 6, in which the size of the font corresponds to the number of times that specific word occurs in the trending video's titles. The colour, in this case, is the algorithm-determined sentiment of that word: red is positive (a polarity score of greater than 0), dark gray is negative (polarity less than 0), and light gray is neutral. The library TextBlob was used for this sentiment score.

From Figure 6, it is apparent right away that this sentiment analysis is hardly a perfect tool. Looking at Figure 6, we can see that some words were classified as either positive or negative in some strange ways. For example, 'green' is labeled as negative despite the fact that colours, by nature, are fairly neutral. Perhaps this is due to its usage in idioms such as "green with envy" or the fact that it can be used to describe someone who looks ill. However, you could also consider 'green' in reference to environmental causes, which would instead mean this word should be positive. Similarly, 'live' is classified as positive, most likely due to it being interpreted in reference to being alive rather than a live broadcast, which is more likely considering the context of this dataset.

Regardless of the few odd sentiment assignments, there does not seem to be any specific trend to the sentiment of the words used. While there is more positivity present in the top-used words than negativity, there is not enough of either to make a sound conclusion. Instead, we can see that titles are overwhelmingly dominated by neutral words, such as names ('Taylor Swift', 'SNL', 'Trump') and basic nouns ('video', 'food', 'cat'). Unfortunately, this doesn't give us much to go on for building our own titles; however, it does give us a sense of which topics are more likely to trend.

In order to get a better understanding of trending titles, I trained a Markov chain generator on the titles in order to generate my own. To add an extra element of fun, I also chose a random channel from the list of those who have trended and assigned these to these generated titles. So, what should be the next trending video title according to a Markov chain generator?

While creating this generator was initially for fun rather than for performing some sort of in-depth analysis, I found that certain patterns seemed to stick in these generated titles that could be reflective of the training dataset. For example, many of the generated titles were either completely capitalized or contained words that were completely capitalized; this suggests using all-caps may be a regular occurrence in actual trending titles, since the generator picked up on this and replicated it.

Similarly, almost every single time I ran my generation, at least one of the titles included an exclamation mark. Upon further inspection of this, I found that, of the 34,110 titles in my sampled dataset, 5,231 contained an exclamation mark; this accounts for approximately 15% of the trending titles. In comparison, question marks appeared in about 7.5% and periods made up approximately 12%. Hence, my intuition was right and exclamation points are indeed one of the top-used end of sentence punctuation marks.

While titles are meant to draw users in once they see your video, tags are used to make sure that users see your video in the first place. The tags of a video are more hidden than the title, but they still contain useful information about the contents of the video itself. Since titles are more visible and meant to be a draw for viewers, they are not necessarily representative of the actual contents: for example, consider the clickbait titles above. Hence, I wanted to see if the top words used in titles were in any way related to the tags.

I started by getting a general understanding of what words were most frequent in tags, similar to the way I did for titles in Figure 6. I did this by gathering the top 200 tags and mapping them to a word cloud, where the size of each word represents the number of times it appeared. The color mapping is the same as in Figure 6, where red is positive, dark gray is negative, and light gray is neutral. The results can be seen in Figure 7.

Since I was not able to translate this column due to my translation limitations, I used the unsampled dataset for this analysis. Interestingly, despite the fact that this dataset contains 78 unique languages, a majority of the top-used tags were in English. This reflects the findings of Question 2 above, where English was the majority language for 3 out of the 5 trending pages and the second-most popular in the other 2.

Once again, we can see some odd choices by the sentiment analysis tool. However, ignoring this, we can see that the breakdown of sentiment is very similar to that of the word usage in the titles. The top 200 words in titles were approximately 7.5% positive, whereas in tags the top 200 were approximately 5.5% positive. For words classified as negative, titles included approximately 3% and tags about 1.5%. Neutral words, in both subsets, were the majority.

Now, out of the top 200 words in titles and the top 200 words in tags, 105 of them are the same (in the sampled dataset). So, in regards to my initial question, it seems that titles and tags are indeed very related. In fact, of the top 5 most-frequent words in both titles and tags, 3 of them were the same. The breakdown of these words is shown in Table 2.

Interestingly, the top-used word in tags was 'funny', which did not appear at all in the top 200 words used for titles. However, of the top 5 words in titles, every single one of them appeared in the top 200 tags. Despite this, there was no obvious trend in which words appeared in titles but not tags and vice versa. Words that were included in titles but not in tags include 'death', 'commercial', and 'trailers'. On the other hand, some words included in tags but not in titles were 'cats' (titles included 'cat' rather than the plural), 'education', and 'television'.

That being said, the main takeaway from this analysis is which topics are likely to trend; for example, cat videos seem to be a good choice based on the number of times 'cat' appears in trending titles. Specific names to include may be Donald Trump, Taylor Swift, and Logan Paul, since these seem to be fairly common occurences in either the titles or tags. For more general ideas, however, it seems as though food, eating, and music are all popular. Another takeway is the fact that, out of all punctuation, it seems as though exclamation marks are the way to go.

Question #4

When should you publish your video?

If your goal is to trend, is there a specific time and/or day of the week that will help to achieve this? That is the goal of this section of the data analysis. In particular, we want to see if there are any trends to whether or not the day you publish a video will affect the likelihood it trends and/or the time it takes to make the trending page.

First, let's determine whether the day a video is published impacts the likelihood of it trending. For example, is there a certain weekday that is best to publish your video in order for it to trend? If we break down the number of videos by the weekday they made the trending page as well as the weekday they were published, we may get an idea of whether or not there is a relation between weekday and the chances of trending. Not only that, but we may be able to draw a conclusion on which day is best to publish your video if you wish to trend. I began tackling some of these questions using Figure 8, which (for reference) uses the unsampled dataset.

In Figure 8, we can see that the total number of trending videos on each weekday is fairly uniform. The only exceptions to this are Friday and Sunday, which both have slightly fewer trending videos than the other 5 weekdays. On the other hand, the number of videos published on each day is slightly more erratic. Between Monday and Wednesday, the number of trending videos published on these days goes down. Wednesday and Thursday have the fewest number of videos published on these days total, with Wednesday being the worst of the two. Then, the number trends upward to Friday and stays fairly consistent over the weekend.

From this, we can see that very few trending videos are published on Wednesday's and Thursday's despite the fact that the number of trending videos stays pretty stable regardless of the weekday. That being said, this could be due to the fact that fewer videos in general are published in the middle of the week. Unfortunately, since our data is limited to trending videos only, we have no way of knowing how many videos total are published on each day of the week. Hence, we do not have enough information to verify this or make any solid conclusions. Still, it may be best to avoid these two days when deciding when to make your video live.

Since it is difficult to determine the likelihood of trending depending on the day a video was published, let's instead see if there is any correlation between the day a video was published and how long it takes to make the trending page. In order to calculate this value, which we will hereby call the wait time, we took the day a video made the trending page and subtracted the day it was published. We converted this value to the approximate number of days between the two dates in order to simplify the output of this calculation. Once we had this value for every single video in our unsampled dataset, we could proceed with the rest of our analysis.

Figure 9 shows the breakdown of both the average and median wait time for the videos that trended on each day in our dataset. From this, we can very clearly see that the average is driven up by a small subset of videos, since the median wait time is consistently much lower than the average. Despite this, it seems as though the average is currently on a downward trend and, in fact, may be becoming more consistent overall.

From the start of the trending videos in 2017 until Febuary of 2018, there were large dips and spikes in the average wait time. For example, the largest spike was on January 31st, 2018, where 2 videos from 2007, a video from 2008, and 3 videos from 2011 all made the trending page. A few of the oldest videos that trended on this day are the following:

Jeffrey Lewis The Legend of The Fall

A video published on 2007-03-05 by Dav Hum that made the trending page in Germany

Bill is Dead The Fall

A video published on 2007-06-25 by georgegargan that made the trending page in Germany

Sega Game Gear Commercial "Creamed Spinach"

A video published on 2012-12-11 by VideoGameAds that made the trending page in the US

I noticed right away that, of the top 10 oldest videos that trended on January 31st, 9 of them trended only in Germany. In fact, 6 of them were music videos by a British band called 'The Fall,' and another was about the lead singer of this band, Mark E. Smith. When I looked into why this was the case, I found that Smith passed away on January 24th, 2018, a mere 7 days before [source]. Hence, this explains the mass resurfacing of these videos, many of which were over a decade old at the time of trending.

However, in the last 5 weeks, the intensity of the dips and spikes of the average wait time seems to have smoothed out. Of course, it is entirely possible an extreme event such as what happened on January 31st may drive this wait time up once again. Still, the most recent weeks were much more stable than any other span of time in my dataset. That being said, the average wait time does not seem to show any significant evidence to suggest that publishing time impacts the time it takes a video to trend.

While the average wait time did not tell us much about whether or not publishing time impacts how long a video takes to trend, I was interested in finding out whether or not the median wait time told a different story. Since the average was so significantly higher than the median in Figure 9 above, it was extremely difficult to draw any firm conclusions on the median without looking at it seperately. Hence, in order to take a closer look at the median wait time, Figure 10 is the same as Figure 10 but does not include the average; in this graph, the days that are highlighted with a darker gray are the weekend.

Right away, there seems to be a much more predictable pattern to the median wait time in comparison to the average. Quite consistently, the median wait time seems to dip in the middle of the week and spike around the weekend. Of course, there are exceptions to this general pattern; once again, January 31st shows a larger-than-normal spike in the wait time in the middle of the week. Still, this pattern is consistent enough that I believe it is fair to draw conclusions from the median, unlike with the average wait time.

Hence, according to Figure 10, videos that trend in the middle of the week tend to have a short wait time than those that trend on the weekend. The median around this time is usually between 1 and 1.25 days; so, if you want to have a chance at trending faster, this suggests it is best to post your video somewhere between a Sunday and a Tuesday.

It is worth mentioning that I originally tried to look at both Figure 9 and 10 from the perspective of the publish date rather than the trending date. However, due to the large variety of publish dates in my dataset, this resulted in a graph that was messy and much harder to draw conclusions from. Since anomalies such as January 31st existed, the graphs contained large gaps which skewed the wait time overall. While focusing on trending date may not provide as strong of a conclusion, at least it is easy to interpret.

Question #5

Do views actually matter?

According to YouTube's own guidelines, there are 4 determining factors that go into deciding whether or not a video will make the trending page [source]:

  1. The video is appealing to a wide range of viewers.
  2. The video is not misleading, clickbaity or sensational.
  3. The video captures the breadth of what’s happening on YouTube and in the world.
  4. The video is surprising or novel.
They claim the things that are considered when making this decision include, but are not limited to, the number of views, how fast the views were accumulated, whether the views come from external sources, as well as the age of the video [source]. Since views seem to be the focus of this decision process, let's explore some of the anomalies in this regard. Along the way, we may be able to gather some information about what else goes into making a video trend.

First, let's take a look at the videos that performed the worst in regards to views. We saw in Question 1 above that, in general, music videos perform the best when it comes to total views, whereas the 'Shows', 'Nonprofits & Activism', and 'Travel & Events' categories tend to have the fewest views overall. That being said, of the worst-viewed trending videos, which category is the most prevelant? This might give us some information as to why such low-performing videos made the trending page to begin with. In order to find this out, I took the top 1,000 videos with the lowest views from my unsampled dataset and counted how many fell into each category.

Figure 11 shows us that, out of the 1,000 lowest-viewed trending videos, a majority of them fell under the People & Blogs category. However, the top 4 all came fairly close to tieing in this regard: the top category was People & Blogs with 166 videos, however, next in line was Sports with 162, a difference of only 4 videos. In third place was Entertainment with 148, and the fourth-most unviewed category was News & Politics with 145. The remaining categories all had 72 or less videos total, which shows us that these top 4 all perform much worse in general when compared to the other categories.

The results of this analysis surprised me. When I originally tried this and took only the top 100 lowest-viewed trending videos, I found that News & Politics was, by far, the most common category. This made sense to me, since it YouTube would want to push breaking news to the trending page before it gets views; once a news video is seen by enough people, chances are that specific story is already out-of-date. So, making news videos trend regardless of views makes sense. However, when I changed this number and looked at the 1,000 videos instead, this changed: People & Blogs now had the most unviewed trending videos.

I cannot think of a clear justification as to why this might be the case. Unlike with News & Politics, it does not seem as though videos in the People & Blogs category are time-sensitive. One possible explanation I came up with was that YouTube has certain creators that they push to the trending page regardless of views. However, upon further exploration of this possibility, this theory was unsupported. There was no single creator that seemed to be overepresented in this category, or in any other for that matter. The same justification that exists for News & Politics can be applied to Sports, but the justification for People & Blogs as well as Entertainment leaves me stumped.

In order to investigate this further, let's take a look at the median wait time of the People & Blogs category in comparison to all other categories. While we already showed that it is unlikely that YouTube is pushing certain creators to the trending page in this category, it may be the case that these videos tend to trend faster than any other category. This could explain why a majority of the most unviewed videos fell under this category, since if they trend faster they may not have the same chance to rack up views.

According to Figure 12, the median wait time for People & Blogs is consistently lower than the combined median wait time for all other categories. This suggests that that the reason this category has so many unviewed videos may be that they tend to be pushed to the trending page faster than other categories. Of course, there may be a certain category that is driving the other mean up. Still, this provides a reasonable justification for why this category is so overepresented in the lowest-viewed 1,000 trending videos.

As an aside, let's look at the worst-viewed videos on each country's trending page. Out of the unsampled dataset, there were 70 unique videos with less than 1,000 views at the time of trending. These videos trended in Germany, France, the UK, and the United States, but interestingly not a single one of them made the trending page in Canada. The lowest-viewed Canadian video, on the other hand, had exactly 1,000 views at the time of trending. The lowest viewed videos (at the time of trending) for each country are displayed in Table 3.

Now, a majority (3 out of 5) of these videos comes from the 'News & Politics' category. What makes a controversial video?

Now, our analysis thus far has focused on what you should do, and we have yet to really discuss what you shouldn't do. Obviously, all of the videos on our dataset did indeed make the trending page, so they've each done something right in that regard. That being said, while unpopular or contraversial videos may bring in views, they are not necessarily the best if you wish to recieve positive feedback. So, let's look at what topics you should avoid when making videos.

First, let's take a look at the most positively and negatively received trending videos. To do this analysis, we first determined the breakdown of likes to dislikes by normalizing these two columns; hence, rather than having the actual number of likes and dislikes, we instead had the percentage breakdown of likes to dislikes for each video. This was done on the sampled dataset, since we inteded to perform a similar title analysis as shown in Question 3.

Once we had the likes to dislikes ratio, we filtered out the videos with less than 50,000 views. Since some videos made the trending page despite having limited views, these seemed to skew our ratios since a single dislike was weighted much heavier when there were fewer likes to balance it out. Since our assumption was that a contraversial video would receive quite a few views, and in order to accomodate for this unbalance, we chose to remove any relatively unviewed videos from our consideration.
