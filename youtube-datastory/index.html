Question #1

What genre should your video be?

The trending page of YouTube is broken down into 15 different categories. One possible subset of the more popular genres, in no particular order, is as follows:

  • Comedy
  • Gaming
  • Entertainment
  • Music
  • Howto & Style
  • Sports
With such a large variety of options to choose from, which category should you make content for? Obviously, the most obvious answer would be to make content for the genre you are passionate about and talented in. After all, if you are hopeleslly tonedeaf, it may not be worth your time to persue a career in music.

That being said, let us approach this problem as though each genre of video is equally likely and enjoyable. Considering this, what factors would influence our decision on which category to aim for?

One of the first things that comes to mind when hearing that a video is 'popular' on YouTube is likely the number of views a specific video gets. It is unlikely, for example, that a video with under 500 views would be considered 'popular' by the general YouTube audience. Since the goal of creating content for this platform is most likely to make these types of 'popular' videos, it makes sense to start by looking at this factor for each category.

As can be seen above, the Music category far outweighs the other genres in terms of overall views. In comparison to the second-most viewed category (Entertainment), Music has approximately 30-million more views; this is nearly double the number of views! With some thought, this result is fairly unsurprising. First off, music is universably shareable. This is especially true due to the lack of a language barrier when it comes to music in comparison to other forms of digital media. Although you may not understand the lyrics of a particular song, that by no means impacts your ability to enjoy it. As a result, it is not uncommon for music videos to trend in multiple countries. For example, here are just a few of the 55 different music videos that made the trending page of every country in our dataset:

By BTS (방탄소년단)

End Game ft. Ed Sheeran, Future

By Taylor Swift

Man of the Woods (Official Video)

By Justin Timberlake
In comparison, the next most universally shared category was Entertainment with only 29 videos that made every trending page, which is almost half of the number of music videos that met the same condition. The category 'Film & Animation' had 15 of these videos, and all the remaining categories had 10 or less. Of the fifteen possible categories, 3 of them had the minimum value of 1. This helps to illustrate our idea that music videos are more universally shareable in comparison to the other categories.

Music videos also have one of the highest replayability factors in comparison to the other categories. It is not uncommon for people without access to some other music streaming service to make use of YouTube's multitude of musical content. After all, this is the reason why the paid-for-subscription-service YouTube Red has an option to stream music in a manner not unlike Spotify. This is just another explanation for why this category dominates the other categories in terms of views.

Despite the Music category getting the most views overall, the future is not-so-bleak for the other categories. Figure 2 below illustrates the number of views (per day) of music videos in comparison to all other categories. It is worth mentioning that this figure was constructed using the sampled version of our dataset. This helped to normalize the results, since only 5 videos from each category (per day) were included. I tried constructing this same graph with both versions of my dataset, and I found this one to be the most interesting.

Figure 2 shows that the views of the videos in the Music category as well as all other categories (when considered as a whole) are on the rise. In fact, the views trend line for 'Other' seems to have a slightly steeper slope than the one for 'Music'. This shows that, while music videos may get more views overall than all other categories, views in general are on an upward trend. As a result, the future of views looks promising regardless of the video category you choose.

However, views are not all that matters when it comes to choosing a category that fits you best. For example, you may be someone who values user comments and direct communication with your audience more than simply the number of views. On the other hand, you may be heavily impacted by negative feedback, so you'd prefer to pick a category that has mostly positive feedback. These are other factors, besides views, that may impact your decision for which category to produce content for.

Figure 3 below shows the breakdown of user interaction per category, as described by the average number of comments, likes, and dislikes on the videos. Since the views of every category far outweighed any other interaction factor (and since I've already heavily discussed views above), I chose not to include the percentage of user interaction that comes from views. This helped give more insight into the other factors.

Using Figure 3, we can answer the above hypothetical examples. If user comments are an important factor for you when making your videos, then the category 'Nonprofits & Activism' is for you. This can be seen by the fact that this category has 21% of its user interaction coming from comments, which is the most out of any other category. Interestingly, this category also has a higher than average percentage of dislikes, coming in at 14.5%. As a result, this may not be the best category for the second scenario where negative feedback is extremely undesirable. Instead, the best categories for this scenario would be 'Pets & Animals' (with only 1.7% of user interaction coming from dislikes) or 'Comedy' (which has only 2.7%).

Question #2

Which language should your title be?

Depending on the language you speak, it's possible that your video may not trend. After all, videos in languages that do not fall into the majority limits the potential size of the audience, which goes against the fundamental idea behind the trending page. Trending videos are meant to target as wide of an audience as possible, so it makes sense to publish a video in one of the top languages if you want to increase your chances of making this list.

Hence, let's start by looking at the diversity of each trending page. In the context of this analysis, diversity is measured by the number of unique lanugages detected for the titles of each country's page. Overall, my sampled dataset had a total of 78 unique languages used for the titles. Hence, the number of unique languages is most likely actually much larger. Still, I was able to get a good understanding of the breakdown of the majority languages, and this analysis can be seen in Table 1.

In order to get a more in-depth analysis of the language use in each trending page, I took the top 5 languages from each country and mapped the percentage breakdown of the resulting shortened language list for each of the trending pages. Since there was a lot of overlap between these top 5, I ended up only looking at 12 different languages for each trending page:

  • English
  • French
  • Chinese
  • Hindi
  • German
  • Turkish
  • Russian
  • Arabic
  • Haitian Creole
  • Korean
  • Japanese
  • Portuguese
